Lily of the Valley Care Guide

Everything you need to grow Convallaria majalis successfully in your garden

⚠️ Critical Toxicity Warning — Read Before Planting

ALL parts of Lily of the Valley are highly toxic to humans, dogs, and cats — including the leaves, flowers, berries, roots, seeds, and even the water in a vase. The plant contains over 38 cardiac glycosides including convallatoxin, which disrupts heart electrical signalling and can cause vomiting, low heart rate, and in severe cases, death.

If any part is ingested by a person: Call Poison Control immediately (USA: 1-800-222-1222). Do not induce vomiting unless directed.

If a pet ingests any part: Contact an emergency vet or the ASPCA Poison Control (888-426-4435) immediately.

Always wash hands thoroughly after handling the plant. Do not let children play near it unsupervised.

Choosing a Site

Lily of the Valley is a woodland plant — choose a spot that mimics its natural habitat. It thrives where few other flowering plants succeed.

  • Light: Partial to full shade is ideal. Morning sun with afternoon shade works well. Avoid full, direct afternoon sun — it scorches the leaves and reduces flowering.
  • Aspect: North or east-facing borders under deciduous trees are perfect. The tree canopy provides summer shade while allowing winter light to reach the dormant roots.
  • Space: Lily of the Valley spreads enthusiastically. In ideal conditions it can colonise a wide area. Plant in a naturally bordered area or use root barriers if you want to contain it.
  • Temperature: Very cold-hardy (USDA Zones 2–9). Requires a period of winter dormancy and cold to bloom well the following spring.

Soil Preparation

The right soil makes an enormous difference to the vigour and longevity of your plants.

  • Type: Moist, well-drained soil rich in humus (decomposed organic matter). Mimics the leaf mould layer of a woodland floor.
  • pH: Slightly acidic to neutral — pH 6.0 to 7.0. Strongly alkaline soils should be amended.
  • Preparation: Dig in generous amounts of well-rotted leafmould, garden compost, or bark chippings before planting to improve drainage and organic content.
  • Drainage: While the plant loves moisture, it will rot in waterlogged soil. If your soil is heavy clay, add horticultural grit and raise the bed slightly.
  • Avoid: Sandy, dry, or nutrient-poor soils unless heavily amended. The plant will survive but will not thrive or spread.

Planting

When to Plant

The best time to plant is autumn (October–November), when the rhizomes are dormant and will settle in before winter. You can also plant in early spring (February–March) before new growth begins, though autumn-planted rhizomes typically establish better.

How to Plant

  • Purchase bare-root rhizomes ("pips") or pot-grown plants from a reputable nursery.
  • Dig a trench or individual holes about 3–4 cm (1.5 inches) deep.
  • Place rhizomes horizontally with the growing tip (the pale, pointed bud) just at or slightly below the soil surface.
  • Cover with soil and gently firm down. Water in well.
  • Apply a layer of leaf mould or bark mulch 2–3 cm thick.

Spacing

Space rhizomes 15 cm (6 inches) apart for a good display within 2–3 seasons. For immediate impact, plant more closely at 8–10 cm. For naturalising a larger area, 20 cm spacing is acceptable — the colony will fill in over time.

Watering

  • Keep soil consistently moist during the growing season (spring and early summer).
  • Water thoroughly during dry spells — the roots are shallow and dry out quickly.
  • Mulch generously in spring to retain moisture and keep roots cool. Leaf mould, bark chips, or garden compost all work well.
  • In hot, dry summers, water once or twice per week. Morning watering reduces the risk of fungal disease.
  • After leaves die back in autumn, reduce watering. The plant is dormant and needs minimal moisture.
  • Never let water pool — waterlogged roots will rot.

Feeding

  • Lily of the Valley is not a hungry plant. Over-fertilising promotes leafy growth at the expense of flowers.
  • Apply a balanced, slow-release granular fertiliser in early spring as new growth emerges. A handful worked lightly into the soil surface is sufficient.
  • Alternatively, topdress with well-rotted garden compost or leafmould each autumn. As this breaks down, it feeds the roots naturally.
  • Avoid high-nitrogen feeds — they encourage soft, floppy growth susceptible to disease.
  • A light feed of liquid tomato fertiliser (high in potassium) in early spring can encourage more prolific flowering.

Seasonal Care Calendar

Follow this guide through the year to keep your Lily of the Valley healthy and flowering reliably.

🌱 Spring

  • Remove winter mulch as shoots emerge
  • Apply slow-release fertiliser
  • Water regularly as flowers develop
  • Enjoy the blooms — April to May
  • Cut flowers for indoor arrangements
  • Plant new pips now if not done in autumn

☀️ Summer

  • Water during dry spells
  • Leave foliage in place — it feeds the roots
  • Watch for slugs on new leaves
  • Divide crowded clumps if needed
  • Red berries appear — admire but do not eat

🍂 Autumn / Fall

  • Leaves yellow and die back — this is normal
  • Cut back dead foliage once fully yellowed
  • Best time to plant new pips
  • Divide and replant crowded clumps
  • Topdress with leafmould or compost

❄️ Winter

  • Plant is fully dormant — roots underground
  • Apply bark mulch as frost protection
  • No watering needed in cold climates
  • Mark planting areas to avoid disturbing roots
  • Plan new plantings for spring

Division & Propagation

Lily of the Valley is easily and freely propagated by dividing its rhizomes. Division also prevents overcrowding, which reduces flowering over time.

  • When: Divide in early autumn (September–October) or early spring before growth begins. Autumn division gives best results.
  • How: Dig up a clump carefully with a fork. Separate individual rhizomes ("pips") by hand — each should have at least one visible growing tip and some attached roots.
  • Replant immediately, at 6-inch spacing, just below the soil surface. Water in well and mulch.
  • Frequency: Divide every 4–5 years, or whenever flowering becomes sparse (usually a sign of overcrowding).
  • Gloves: Wear gloves during handling — the sap can cause skin irritation in some people.
  • Sharing: Excess pips make wonderful gifts for fellow gardeners!

Pests & Problems

Slugs & Snails

The most common pest. They attack young spring shoots and leaves, leaving ragged holes. Use beer traps, copper tape, or organic slug pellets. Hand-picking at night is also effective.

Botrytis (Grey Mould)

A fungal disease that causes grey, fuzzy patches on leaves and flowers — especially in cool, damp conditions with poor air circulation. Remove affected parts immediately. Improve airflow and avoid wetting the foliage.

Leaf Spot

Brown or tan spots on the leaves, sometimes with a yellow halo. Usually caused by fungal pathogens. Remove affected leaves and avoid overhead watering. Ensure good air circulation.

Root Rot

Caused by waterlogged soil. Affected plants appear wilted and yellowed despite adequate moisture. Improve drainage and replant in a better-draining location.

Failure to Flower

Common causes: too much sun (scorches and dries roots), overcrowded rhizomes (divide every 4–5 years), too much nitrogen feed, or rhizomes planted too deep. Ensure the growing tip is just at the soil surface.

Deer & Rabbits

Lily of the Valley is generally deer-resistant due to its toxicity, but hungry deer may still browse it in harsh winters. Rabbits occasionally nibble young shoots. Wire protection may be needed in rural areas.
