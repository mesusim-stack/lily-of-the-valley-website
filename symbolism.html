Symbolism & Cultural Meaning

From medieval Christian legend to royal weddings — the rich cultural story of Convallaria majalis

Origins of the Name

The name Convallaria majalis comes from Latin: Convallis meaning "valley" and majalis meaning "of May" or "belonging to the month of May." In English, it became "Lily of the Valley" — though botanically it is not a true lily at all. It belongs to the Asparagaceae family.

Other common names reflect its cultural associations: May bells, muguet (French), Maiglöckchen (German, meaning "May bells"), suzuran (Japanese, 鈴蘭, meaning "bell orchid"), and Our Lady's Tears (Christian tradition).

The phrase "lily of the valley" appears in the King James Bible (Song of Solomon 2:1 — "I am the rose of Sharon, and the lily of the valleys"), though scholars debate whether this referred to the same plant we know today. Nevertheless, this biblical association profoundly shaped the flower's symbolic meaning in Western culture.

Christian & Biblical Symbolism — "Our Lady's Tears"

In Christian legend, Lily of the Valley sprang from the tears shed by the Virgin Mary at the crucifixion of Jesus Christ. This origin story gave the flower its alternative name: "Our Lady's Tears" or "Mary's tears."

In another version of the legend, the flowers grew from the tears Eve wept as she was banished from the Garden of Eden — connecting the flower to themes of loss, redemption, and hope.

Throughout the medieval period, the flower appeared frequently in illuminated manuscripts, religious paintings, and church gardens. Its white bells symbolised purity, humility, and devotion — qualities associated with the Virgin Mary. Monks grew it in monastery physic gardens for both medicinal and spiritual purposes.

In the iconography of saints, Lily of the Valley appears alongside Saint Leonard (patron of prisoners) who, according to legend, fought a dragon in a French forest — and wherever his blood fell, Lily of the Valley grew.

French May Day — La Fête du Muguet

In France, Lily of the Valley is inextricably linked to May 1st — La Fête du Muguet (Festival of the Lily of the Valley). Every May Day, approximately 60 million stems of muguet are bought and gifted across France, making it one of the largest flower-gifting events in the world.

The tradition was formalised in 1561 when King Charles IX of France received a sprig of Lily of the Valley as a lucky charm. Enchanted by both its beauty and its meaning, he decided to offer a sprig to each of the ladies of his court every May Day as a symbol of good luck and spring. The custom spread rapidly through French society.

By the 20th century, the tradition had become a beloved national institution. Famously, French law uniquely permits anyone to sell Lily of the Valley on May 1st without a street vendor's permit — meaning schoolchildren, community groups, and individuals can sell bundles freely. The proceeds often go to charitable causes.

May 1st is also France's Labour Day, and the muguet has become intertwined with both the spring celebration and the workers' rights movement — sometimes appearing on union banners and political posters.

Royal Connections

Lily of the Valley has appeared in some of the most significant royal events of the 20th century:

Queen Elizabeth II — Coronation, 1953

Queen Elizabeth II's coronation bouquet on June 2, 1953, included Lily of the Valley alongside orchids, lily of the valley, stephanotis, freesias, and maidenhair fern. The bouquet was designed by Martin Longman of the Worshipful Company of Gardeners. Lily of the Valley represented purity and happiness on one of the most significant occasions in British history.

Kate Middleton — Royal Wedding, 2011

Catherine Middleton (now Princess of Wales) carried a stunning cascade bouquet of Lily of the Valley for her 2011 wedding to Prince William at Westminster Abbey. Designed by Shane Connolly, the bouquet also included Sweet William (a romantic nod to the groom's name), hyacinth, and myrtle — the latter a tradition stretching back to Queen Victoria's wedding in 1840.

Princess Grace of Monaco — 1956

American actress Grace Kelly, on becoming Princess Grace of Monaco in 1956, chose Lily of the Valley as a key element of her bridal arrangements. Her wedding was one of the most watched events of the era, and the choice of muguet reinforced its status as a flower of elegance and new beginnings.

Norse & Germanic Folklore

In Norse mythology, Lily of the Valley was considered sacred to Ostara (or Eostre), the goddess of spring and dawn — whose name gave us the word "Easter." The flowers were seen as her earthly manifestation: small lanterns she carried to light the way as she transformed winter into spring.

In old Germanic folklore, the flowers were described as fairy lanterns — tiny lights strung by fairies along forest paths to guide travellers on dark nights. Children were warned not to step on the flowers, lest they disturb the fairy folk who depended on them for light.

In some northern European traditions, the appearance of the first Lily of the Valley in spring was treated as an omen of good fortune for the coming year — a living almanac predicting a bountiful summer.

Japanese Culture — Suzuran 鈴蘭

In Japan, Lily of the Valley is known as suzuran (鈴蘭) — literally "bell orchid" — a name that beautifully captures its pendant, bell-like flowers. It is the official flower of Hokkaido, Japan's northernmost island, where it grows wild in mountain meadows.

In Japanese culture, the suzuran symbolises purity, happiness, and the return of joy. Giving someone Lily of the Valley is a gesture of sincere goodwill and a wish for their happiness. The flower appears in traditional Japanese poetry (haiku and tanka) as a symbol of delicate, transient beauty.

The Sapporo Suzuran Festival, held in Hokkaido each spring, celebrates the flower with music, food, and traditional craft markets among the blooming fields.

Victorian Language of Flowers

The Victorian era (1837–1901) saw an elaborate system for communicating emotions through flowers, known as floriography or the "language of flowers." Books such as The Language of Flowers by Kate Greenaway (1884) codified specific meanings for hundreds of blooms.

Lily of the Valley held one of the most profound meanings in this system: "You have made my life complete" — considered the ultimate declaration of love and gratitude. Presenting someone with a sprig of muguet was a deeply intimate gesture, conveying that they had brought joy and fullness to the giver's existence.

Other Victorian meanings associated with the flower included: sweetness, humility, the return of happiness, and purity of heart. It appeared frequently in bridal bouquets, mourning arrangements, and gifts between close friends.

Modern Symbolism

  • National flower of Finland: Officially designated in 1967. It appears on Finnish stamps, folk embroidery, and decorative arts. In Finland, it blooms in late May to June — later than in central Europe due to the northern climate.
  • May birth flower: Along with the hawthorn, Lily of the Valley is one of the traditional birth flowers for the month of May — making it a popular birthday gift for those born in spring.
  • 2nd wedding anniversary flower: In some traditions, Lily of the Valley is the floral symbol for the second wedding anniversary — representing the deepening of a young relationship.
  • Helsinki City Flower: The capital of Finland adopted it as its official city flower.
  • Bulgaria: Lily of the Valley Day (May 1st) is celebrated in Bulgaria similarly to France, with the giving of flowers as tokens of friendship and good luck.
  • Perfume industry: The "muguet" accord is one of the most iconic fragrance notes in perfumery — though the true essential oil cannot be extracted (see Science page).

In Literature & Art

Lily of the Valley appears throughout Western literature and art as a symbol of purity, lost innocence, and transient beauty:

  • Honoré de Balzac — In his 1836 novel Le Lys dans la Vallée (The Lily of the Valley), the flower serves as a central metaphor for the pure, unattainable love of the protagonist Félix for Madame de Mortsauf. The novel is one of the most famous French Romantic works.
  • John Keats — Referenced the flower as an emblem of sweetness and the fleeting nature of spring beauty in his poetry.
  • Pre-Raphaelite Art — Artists such as John Everett Millais incorporated Lily of the Valley into their paintings as a Marian symbol.
  • Christian Dior — The fashion designer kept a sprig of Lily of the Valley in his jacket pocket as a lucky charm before every show. He also wore the flower as a buttonhole on opening days.
  • Russian Fabergé Eggs — Several of the famous Fabergé Easter eggs crafted for the Russian royal family featured Lily of the Valley in enamel and diamonds.
  • Art Nouveau — The sinuous, bell-hung stems of the flower were a favourite motif in Art Nouveau jewellery, glass, and illustration from the 1890s to 1910s.
