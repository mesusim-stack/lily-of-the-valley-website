Discovering Lily of the Valley

Mark Gabriel Susim  |  May 15, 2026

These delicate bell-shaped flowers are celebrated in folklore, literature, and royal traditions for centuries. In this first blog post, I share what drew me to this remarkable plant and why I decided to dedicate an entire website to it.

From its enchanting fragrance — so intense that a single stem can scent an entire room — to its surprising resilience as a woodland groundcover, Lily of the Valley is a plant of contradictions: delicate yet hardy, gentle yet toxic, common in gardens yet steeped in royal history.

Symbolism Across Cultures

Mark Gabriel Susim  |  May 10, 2026

Lily of the Valley is one of the most symbolically loaded flowers in the world. It represents purity in Christian art, happiness in Japanese culture, and good luck in France. On May 1st each year, an estimated 60 million stems change hands across France alone.

The tradition dates to 1561 when King Charles IX received a sprig of muguet as a lucky charm, and declared that the ladies of his court would receive one every May Day. Today it is enshrined in French law — anyone can sell lily of the valley on May 1st without a vendor's licence.

The Science of a Toxic Beauty

Mark Gabriel Susim  |  May 6, 2026

Beneath the innocent bells lies a sophisticated chemical arsenal. Lily of the Valley contains over 38 cardiac glycosides, including convallatoxin — one of the most potent natural heart toxins known. Even the water in a vase of cut stems becomes toxic.

Interestingly, asparagine — the first amino acid ever isolated — was first extracted from Lily of the Valley in 1806. And despite the plant's medicinal use since the 16th century, its compounds are reported to be ten times more potent than strophanthin, a well-known cardiac drug.

A Complete Growing Guide

Mark Gabriel Susim  |  May 3, 2026

Growing Lily of the Valley successfully comes down to understanding its woodland origins. It wants shade, moisture, and rich humus-filled soil — the conditions found under a deciduous tree canopy. Plant rhizomes (called "pips") in fall or early spring, about 6 inches apart, with the growing point just at soil surface.

Once established, it requires very little attention. A light mulch in spring, occasional watering in dry spells, and division every 4–5 years to prevent overcrowding. It will reward you with years of fragrant spring blooms.

Royal Connections & Wedding Bouquets

Mark Gabriel Susim  |  Apr 28, 2026

Few flowers have graced as many royal occasions as Lily of the Valley. Queen Elizabeth II's 1953 coronation bouquet featured it prominently. Kate Middleton carried a cascade of muguet on her wedding day in 2011. Princess Grace of Monaco included it in her 1956 bridal arrangement.

The flower's association with royalty is not merely fashionable — it reflects centuries of symbolism around purity, new beginnings, and the return of happiness. Its tiny white bells make it a natural choice for moments of celebration and solemnity alike.

Photographing Lily of the Valley

Mark Gabriel Susim  |  Apr 20, 2026

Capturing Lily of the Valley on camera is both a joy and a challenge. The tiny bells require a macro lens to show their delicate detail. Shoot on overcast mornings when the light is soft and even — harsh afternoon sun bleaches the white petals and loses all texture.

Use a dark or blurred background (f/8–f/11 with a tight depth of field) to make the flowers pop. Wet leaves and morning dew add magic to the composition. A tripod is essential for sharp macro shots at low apertures.

Exploring the 10 Major Cultivars

Mark Gabriel Susim  |  Apr 14, 2026

Not all Lily of the Valley plants are alike. Breeders have developed dozens of named cultivars over the centuries, each with distinct features — from the giant blooms of Fortin's Giant to the blush-pink bells of Rosea, the cream-striped leaves of Albostriata, and the double flowers of Flore Pleno.

For the strongest fragrance, Reginae is unmatched. For unusual form, Prolificans produces clustered branching stems unlike any other variety. And for golden foliage, Fernwood's Golden Slippers brings a warm glow to shaded corners.
